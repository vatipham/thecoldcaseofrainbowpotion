Case Summary

Synopsis

Chris is sixteen when she gets the diagnosis: Wrongful Orientation Disorder. By morning, she's a patient at Ward 4-C of the Office of Social Hygiene, a clinic that promises to make her "whole again." First, they correct how she looks. They take out her piercings. They laser off her tattoos. They give her a new wardrobe and force her into a "feminine" posture, one teacup at a time.

Foreign BodiesLogged, bagged, sealed.
Dermal CorrectionCalled "restoration."
Wardrobe ReassignmentNothing she chose stays.
Comportment TrainingGraded each morning.

What Chris notices next is how other girls in 4-C would go quieter, duller, less vibrant each week — as if she could witness souls escaping their bodies. Slowly but surely, she feels it too.

Then she finds a freight elevator the intake forms never mentioned. Below the clinic is a vault that shouldn't exist: a hidden greenhouse, glowing with every color drained out of every patient, alive and growing. Inside it, for the first time since she was admitted, Chris feels something again — joy, relief, herself. Then she hears the door. She hides.

Doctor M walks in with a business partner, and Chris listens. Rainbow Potions are not medical waste. They are bottled, graded, and sold at extraordinary prices to rich clients who can't feel joy anymore. Now Chris stands at a choice: leave this institution in silence, or get everyone out.

The Extraction

The real treatment happens below the surface: Rainbow Extraction, once a day, while she's restrained on a table. What comes out of Chris isn't blood. It's violet, gold, green, glowing from somewhere inside her. And every time, it's the worst pain she's ever felt.

From the Filmmaker

Director/Producer's Statement

Vati Pham Director/Producer/Writer
Erasing queerness means erasing lives.

As a queer filmmaker, my mission in storytelling is highlighting struggles of queer communities around the world. I was involved in Ban Conversion Therapy Kentucky during COVID-19, and I want to tell this important story of oppression that queer folx are dealing with worldwide. Unjust healthcare borrows the language of medicine to make coercion look like care. The Cold Case of Rainbow Potion takes that structure literally. I wanted the audience to feel the same pain and suffering Chris and millions of queer youth feel.

— Written for development, casting, and festival submission.
